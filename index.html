About

Nestled in between the luscious green hills of the Buhoma village in Kanungu district of southern Uganda, the Bwindi Impenetrable Forest National Park is home to about 400 Mountain Gorillas– a species only found in this part of the world. Mountain Gorillas are a species of Gorillas, Gorilla beringei beringei, that can only survive in their natural habitat. To witness the lives of these majestic creatures in their own territory is an experience of a lifetime. The Gorilla Tracking Safari Ltd offers excellent trekking experiences for guests willing to venture into the impenetrable forests! Visit us in Bwindi to learn more about the region, to experience the beauty of the Bwindi rainforest, and of course, to meet a wonderful family of mountain gorillas, our distant cousins. We are all 97.7% Gorilla!



Get Involved


Go Gorilla Trekking

To assure that all our guests get a chance to see the mountain gorillas, we facilitate two types of treks:
Short trek: After a jeep ride to the trekking site, guests will have to walk for about an hour and a half on relatively easy terrain before stumbling upon one of the gorilla families.
Long trek: On our longer treks, guest trek up relatively steeper trails for about 2 to 3 hours, depending upon where the gorilla family has been located by our experienced trackers.

Assisted Trek

For guests who have disabilities that prevent them from walking on steep or elevated trails, a team of porters are ready to carry you up to the gorilla family. This service is available to guests who, during their trek, feel unwell or are unable to make it to the site. The disabled are given special considerations; it is advisable to let us know about the disabled so we can organise our 991 team that carries you over the hills.

Help A Ranger

By visiting these mountain gorillas, you will be contributing significantly to the conservation of the species. Your visit will provide funds for conservation projects, to create jobs and bring other benefits to the local communities around Bwindi.
Even if you are unable to visit Uganda, you can still play your role in ensuring the safety and efficiency of our trackers by donating the following items:

Gorillas in Uganda

How has the mountain gorilla population in Bwindi changed over the years?
Since the discovery of mountain gorillas in 1902, this species has endured uncontrolled hunting, disease, destruction of its forest habitats, capture of illegal trade, and even war. It was feared that the Mountain Gorilla would go extinct in the same century it was discovered in.This drastic drop in numbers prompted conservation efforts around the region. This species has been intensely monitored since the 1950s.

  • 1997

    300 (52 habituated)

    During the first proper census of this species in 1997, the national park had just begun habituating the mountain gorillas. The population remained at a low of 300 while organizations like IUCN, WWF, and Uganda Wildlife Authority (UWA) began establishing rules for conservation.

  • 2002

    320 (72 habituated)

    In 2002, a positive trend was observed as there was an additional 20 individuals added to the gorilla families in Bwindi. More number of juvenile and adult gorillas began to get habituated as well.

  • 2006

    302 (76 habituated)

    The census of 2006 revealed that the total number of mountain gorillas had dropped, due to reasons unidentified, but the number of habituated gorillas continued to grow indicating that the efforts of the park rangers were moving ahead successfully.

  • 2011

    400 (168 habituated)

    The given census shows that all the efforts being made have started to pay off. The Mountain Gorilla population in Bwindi has reached the 400 individuals mark, and hopefully, will continue to grow as conservation laws and efforts continue to be enforced.

Nyampazi

Baby

The youngest member of the Mubare gorilla family, Nyampazi is the child of Karungi. He is always curious about the visitors in his territory, and does not mind posing for the camera!

  • Date of Birth: July 2014
  • Location: Uganda
  • Family: MUBARE

Karungi

Mother

As a protective mother and a reserved female gorilla in general, Karungi does not like it when guests get too close to her or her baby. Guests often see her picking out leaves and other fruits for both herself and her little boy, Nyampazi.

  • Date of Birth: July 2014
  • Location: Uganda
  • Family: MUBARE

Kanyonyi

Silverback

As the Silverback gorilla, Kanyoni is the alpha male of the Mubare family. Responsible to protect and defend his group, the silverback is always alert and observing. With a mean weight of 195 kilograms and a height that reaches up to 6 feet, silverbacks like Kanyoni are one of the most powerful of the Great Apes.

  • Date of Birth: July 2014
  • Location: Uganda
  • Family: MUBARE

Kyirinvi

Juvenile male

Males tend to mature at an even greater age than females, and Kyirinvi is an example of this in the Mubare family.

  • Date of Birth: July 2014
  • Location: Uganda
  • Family: MUBARE

Twesiima

Juvenile female

Twseiima is the only female in the group who is yet to bear a child. Females become sexually mature around seven or eight years old but don’t begin to breed until a couple of years later. Once a female begins to breed, she’ll likely give birth to only one baby every four to six years, and only three or four over her entire lifetime. Until then, Twesiima will remain a juvenile.

  • Date of Birth: July 2014
  • Location: Uganda
  • Family: MUBARE

Kikombe

Adult male

Kikombe is a fully grown adult male in the Mubare family. Although he exhibits great strength, he is still inferior to the silverback of the family.

  • Date of Birth: July 2014
  • Location: Uganda
  • Family: MUBARE
